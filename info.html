insights, heritage, and future developments

Preserving Tradition

Singapore’s hawker culture has long been at the heart of daily life, offering more than just affordable meals, it reflects the country’s multicultural roots. In recognition of its cultural value, hawker culture was inscribed on the UNESCO Representative List of the Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity in 2020. This honour underscores the importance of safeguarding traditional food practices passed down through generations.

Many hawkers are elderly individuals who have spent decades perfecting recipes rooted in family or regional traditions. As they retire, there is growing concern about the loss of culinary knowledge and heritage. To address this, the government and community initiatives are working to document recipes, oral histories, and cooking techniques.

Hawker centres also continue to play a vital role as communal spaces where people of different backgrounds come together, fostering social interaction and a shared sense of identity over a simple, hearty meal.

Modernisation & Innovation

While tradition remains important, hawker culture is also evolving with the times. A new generation of young hawkers, often referred to as hawkerpreneurs, is bringing fresh energy and creativity into the scene. These individuals may be professionally trained chefs, former office workers, or passionate food lovers who are reviving traditional dishes with modern twists or introducing entirely new flavours. Many of them actively market their food on Instagram and TikTok, leveraging digital platforms to build loyal followings.

Hawker centres themselves are also embracing technology, with more stalls accepting cashless payments and partnering with food delivery platforms to reach a wider audience. These changes have helped keep hawker culture relevant, especially among younger consumers who value convenience and digital accessibility.

Even as innovation takes place, there's a conscious effort to maintain the spirit of hawker food—affordable, accessible, and rooted in Singapore’s identity.

Future & Sustainability

Looking ahead, the future of hawker culture lies in finding a balance between sustainability, innovation, and heritage preservation. With increasing awareness of environmental issues, many hawkers are shifting toward more sustainable practices—such as using biodegradable packaging, reducing food waste, and offering plant-based meal options.

Supportive government programs, like the NEA’s Incubation Stall Programme, aim to ensure that aspiring hawkers have the resources and guidance needed to succeed, keeping the trade alive. At the same time, public events such as the Singapore Food Festival, hawker exhibitions, and community-led workshops continue to celebrate and promote hawker food as a cultural treasure.

As hawker culture adapts to the changing times, its survival depends not just on nostalgic appreciation but on practical efforts to sustain it by making it attractive to the next generation, environmentally responsible, and globally recognised, while keeping its heart and soul intact.

Hawker Stories

Shared memories from people who love hawker culture

Morning Fuel
Hainanese Chicken Rice, Maxwell Food Centre (Tian Tian)

Every Sunday, my dad and I would swing by Maxwell for Tian Tian's chicken rice before football training. We’d share quiet bites amidst the weekday crowd, and he always praised the chili that just hit the right sweet-heat. It's a taste that still reminds me of his smile.

After-School Ritual
Char Kway Teow, Old Airport Road Food Centre

After school, my friends and I would head straight to Old Airport Road for our $3 plates of Char Kway Teow. Greasy fingers, shared laughs, and the clatter of chopsticks — those afternoons tasted like freedom.

Rainy Day Comfort
Laksa, Queensway Shopping Centre

Whenever it rained, Mum would take me to the laksa stall in Queensway. The steam would fog up my glasses, and the spicy broth warmed more than just my stomach.

Late-Night Bite
Satay, Lau Pa Sat

After long nights at work, my colleagues and I would head to Lau Pa Sat for satay. Smoke in the air, peanut sauce on our fingers, and laughter echoing in the night, it was our kind of happy hour.

Study Breaks
Roti Prata, The Roti Prata House

In university, my friends and I lived on teh tarik and crispy prata during exam season. We'd brainstorm answers over curry and promise to meet again after the chaos.

First Date
Hokkien Mee, Geylang Lor 29

My partner and I shared a plate of Hokkien Mee under the stars on our first date. We still joke that it was the sambal that sealed the deal.

Weekend Tradition
Nasi Lemak, Changi Village

Every Saturday, we’d cycle to Changi Village for nasi lemak. The coconut rice, crispy chicken, and sea breeze made the 10km ride worth it.

Grandma’s Favourite
Mee Rebus, Tekka Centre

Grandma loved the mee rebus at Tekka. Every visit felt like a celebration, with her insisting we finish the gravy down to the last drop.

Birthday Breakfast
Kaya Toast, Ya Kun Kaya Toast

For my birthday every year, Dad would take me for kaya toast and soft-boiled eggs before school. Simple, but perfect.

Lunch with Colleagues
Fish Soup, Amoy Street Food Centre

Workdays were made better by a quick fish soup run with my colleagues. The fresh broth always felt like a mid-day reset.

Festive Feels
Popiah, East Coast Lagoon Food Village

During Chinese New Year, we’d gather at East Coast Lagoon for popiah. Rolling them ourselves made it even more special.

Comfort Food
Bak Chor Mee, Bedok 85

Whenever I felt down, a bowl of bak chor mee from Bedok 85 was my cure. The vinegar tang never failed to lift my mood.

Family Bonding
Oyster Omelette, Newton Food Centre

Every family outing ended with oyster omelette at Newton. We’d argue over who got the crispiest bits.

Exam Season
Wanton Mee, Tiong Bahru Market

My study group swore by the wanton mee at Tiong Bahru. It was our reward for surviving another paper.

Midnight Cravings
BBQ Stingray, Chomp Chomp

On sleepless nights, we’d head to Chomp Chomp for BBQ stingray. The sambal, lime, and smoky char made it worth losing sleep over.
